Experience Design: Totem Journey

Making indigenous culture accessible through immersive storytelling at UBC MOA

This project proposes an interactive experience for the Museum of Anthropology at UBC. Given an (intentionally) ill-defined brief, we conducted field research, framed and reframed the problem, generated user personas, and created a solution in the form of an interactive and discovery-oriented exhibit. Each step in the process was documented and presented with a professional slide deck.

[ Project was done in a team of four without any defined roles. The only divided up part was some of the final execution: for instance I did map illustrations while other people did other illustrations and user profiles.]

Summary of the design proposal

In zone 1, visitors walk through the dimly lit interior of a longhouse and experience video presentations. In zone 2, they can explore on their own in a simulated outdoor area and use totem pole miniatures to activate various audio-video presentations.

Phase 1: project framing

During our first week, we generated research questions, then synthesized our findings into a problem statement. We did our research by visiting the museum in person, talking to staff, reading reviews, and interacting with the museum.

From our research, we found that the museum hadn’t fully developed from its roots as an academic collection. We identified a clear target group we wanted to design for - that being casual visitors who weren’t invested in anthropology.

Phase 2: diverging ideation

To go alongside our problem statement, we developed a few core insights:

“Interactive discoveries immerse the visitor in the story that the artifacts tell, but this is lost when clues and identifiers are unclear."

“As an anthropology museum, MOA’s mandate is to give context on its artifacts, rather than simply display them as pretty objects.”"

“From there, we conducted a live brainstorm, resulting in 3 possible proposals related to wayfinding and organization."

Phase 3: The Solution

The proposed ideas weren’t working for us, so we threw them out and tried again, this time trying not to box ourselves in with tangibility and convention. Our final solution came from reframing our conceptions of the task. The key was to interpret our research findings rather than taking them at face value.
